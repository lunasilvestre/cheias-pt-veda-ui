# Winter 2025-26 Portuguese Flood Events: A Compound Disaster

How sequential storms, saturated soils, and aging infrastructure created the largest water crisis in a generation.

---

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### The Perfect Storm Sequence

    Between December 31, 2025 and February 8, 2026, Portugal was battered by four major Atlantic windstorms in rapid succession. Each brought rain to already-saturated ground. Each overtopped rivers designed for a climate that no longer exists. When the dust settled, 18 people were dead, 12,000 evacuated, and €4 billion in damage documented. The floods revealed what climate scientists had warned for decades: the old infrastructure assumptions are obsolete.

    This story follows the compound disaster from atmospheric preconditions through peak inundation and into recovery.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 1: The Atlantic Engine Warms

<ScrollytellingBlock>
  <Chapter
    center={[-15, 38]}
    zoom={4}
    datasetId='sst-anomaly'
    layerId='sst-anomaly'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Warm Water Fuels the Storm

    In late January 2026, the Atlantic Ocean off Portugal ran 2–3°C warmer than the 1991–2020 average — a 20-year high. Warm sea surface temperatures act like an engine, evaporating more moisture into the lower atmosphere and fueling stronger wind systems. The atmosphere held 7% more moisture than it would have in a pre-warming climate.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.5, 39]}
    zoom={7}
    datasetId='soil-moisture-daily'
    layerId='soil-moisture-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-27'
  >
    ### Soil at Maximum Capacity

    Portugal's soils entered January 2026 already saturated. December and early January had delivered 222% of normal rainfall. Soil moisture in the upper 7 centimeters averaged 0.42 m³/m³, approaching maximum saturation. Every rain drop from Storm Kristin would flow overland or into rivers. The system was primed for flooding.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-12, 39]}
    zoom={5}
    datasetId='ivt'
    layerId='ivt'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Atmospheric River Arrives

    Storm Kristin formed part of a larger pattern: an atmospheric river stretching from the tropics to Portugal. Integrated Water Vapor Transport exceeded 600 kg/m/s, classifying this as extreme. Rivers in the air carry as much water as the Amazon. When they encounter topography, all that moisture precipitates in a narrow zone.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-8.5, 40]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Storm Kristin Makes Landfall

    January 28, 2026. Wind gusts reached 209.8 km/h in Soure — an unprecedented national record. Rainfall rates exceeded 50 mm/day across central Portugal. On saturated soils, this rain ran off directly into rivers. The Mondego, Tejo, Vouga, and Sado all responded rapidly.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-8.5, 40]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='mslp'
    layerId='mslp'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Deep Pressure System

    Kristin's central pressure dropped to 953 hPa. The pressure gradient drove extreme wind speeds — one of the most intense Atlantic windstorms to reach Portugal in modern records. Deep pressure, saturated soils, and vegetation-covered terrain created a perfect recipe for wind damage. Trees uprooted across Coimbra, Leiria, and Guarda districts.
  </Chapter>
</ScrollytellingBlock>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Compounding Cascades

    SST anomaly + saturated soils + atmospheric river = compound disaster. Each element alone would be notable. Together, they created a system incapable of handling the moisture arrival. Hydrologists call this "compound and cascading flooding" — when multiple hazards interact to amplify impacts beyond simple addition.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Unprecedented Wind Records

    Storm Kristin made landfall in Leiria district on January 28, 2026, evening. What followed was the most destructive wind event in Portugal since Hurricane Leslie (2018). Wind gusts reached 209.8 km/h in Soure (Coimbra) — an unprecedented national record, 156 km/h in Leiria, and 115 km/h in Madeira. Structurally designed buildings survived, but infrastructure designed for 120 km/h failed catastrophically.

    By January 30, Tejo river discharge reached **3,706 m³/s** at Vila Franca de Xira — 85% above design capacity. Orange alerts were activated. Municipalities along the Lezíria do Tejo prepared evacuation shelters and sandbagging operations. But the crisis was just beginning.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 4: Human Cost of Kristin

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Deaths and Destruction

    Storm Kristin killed **14 people** in Portugal (6 direct, 8 indirect). Direct causes: falling trees (4), woman swept away by floodwater in Algarve (1), structural collapse (1). Indirect causes: carbon monoxide poisoning from generators during power outages (2), operations and recovery accidents (5), mental health crisis (1).

    **1 million people** lost power. Outages lasted 5–15 days in worst-affected regions. Hospitals, water treatment, and emergency services shifted to backup generators. Communication networks degraded. Kristin alone caused an estimated €2+ billion in damage. Combined with Leonardo and Marta, the total reached €4+ billion — the costliest natural disaster sequence for Portugal in the 21st century.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 5: Storm Leonardo — The Persistent Rainfall

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### A Different Threat Arrives

    Storm Leonardo made landfall on February 3. Unlike Kristin's dramatic winds, Leonardo brought persistent heavy rainfall lasting 3–4 days. Rainfall rates were moderate (20–40 mm/day), not extreme — but the duration mattered. Three consecutive days of rain on saturated soils meant every drop flowed into rivers. Spanish dam operators released stored water downstream to prevent dam overtopping, adding to Portuguese inflows.

    On **February 7, 2026, Tejo discharge reached 6,775 m³/s** — design capacity exceeded by 238%. By February 8, the Lezíria do Tejo floodplain was underwater across **226,764 hectares**. Salvaterra de Magos alone: 49,164 hectares — larger than Lisbon.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### The Dike Rupture — Infrastructure Overwhelmed

    On **February 11**, a critical section of the Mondego River dike near Casais ruptured. Discharge exceeded 2,100 m³/s — the design capacity set in **1986** for a climate that no longer applies. The breach inundated agricultural lands and forced evacuation of **3,000 residents** from Coimbra. Repairs estimated at months. Cost: hundreds of millions of euros.

    By February 6, **1,100+ residents were evacuated** from Alcácer do Sal (Sado basin), Leiria, and surrounding zones. Not brief evacuations — residents sheltered in schools for 1–2 weeks, unsure when they could return.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 7: Storm Marta — The Final Blow

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Sustained Crisis

    Storm Marta arrived February 6–8, just as Leonardo was receding. Additional rainfall (60+ mm in 24 hours), strong winds (120 km/h), rough seas (13-meter waves). Eight of 18 districts remained on orange alert.

    By February 8, Portugal had deployed **26,500 rescue personnel** — one of the largest emergency mobilizations in recent history. Rescuers worked 24-hour shifts in dangerous conditions. Two additional deaths occurred during operations.

    The crisis was so severe that **three municipalities postponed Sunday elections** (February 9) — unprecedented in Portuguese democracy.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 9: The Climate Change Fingerprint

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### What Made This Disaster Possible?

    A World Weather Attribution study found: human-caused climate change **increased rainfall intensity by 11%** in northern Portugal and **36% in the south** for extreme one-day events. Storm Leonardo's totals are now "once-in-a-century" events under current climate. Without climate change, they would be once-in-200-year events.

    Coimbra recorded **1,238 mm** from October 2025 to February 2026 — the wettest October–February in the modern instrumental record, surpassing the previous record set in **1966**.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Future Projections

    Climate models project for Portugal by 2050: annual precipitation will decline 15–30% overall — but extreme precipitation days will intensify. More droughts interspersed with catastrophic flooding. The 2025-26 winter illustrates this pattern perfectly.

    This strengthens the case for: PGRI flood risk plans, EU Floods Directive compliance, infrastructure modernization (21st-century design standards), ecosystem restoration (wetlands as flood buffers), and improved early warning systems.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 10: Recovery and Reconstruction

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Waters Recede, Recovery Begins

    By mid-February, Tejo discharge fell from 6,775 m³/s to 1,498 m³/s. The acute crisis was ending. But recovery had just begun.

    **Housing:** Thousands of homes damaged. Repairs ranged from days to months. **Agriculture:** 69 municipalities under calamity declaration. Livestock losses permanent. Insurance inadequate for most producers. **Infrastructure:** A1 out for months. Dike repairs estimated years. Bridges, water treatment, power stations all damaged.

    On February 10, the government announced a **€2.5 billion emergency package** — housing reconstruction, €750M agricultural relief, infrastructure repair, business continuity support. A €2.5B package for a €4B disaster left significant gaps.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Epilogue: The New Baseline

<Block>
  <Prose>
    By March 2026, recovery was underway but would take months or years. The A1 motorway repaired by June 2026. Dike repairs ongoing through summer. Agricultural recovery: 2–3 seasons. Housing reconstruction: many families still sheltered months later.

    What changed permanently: infrastructure design standards declared obsolete. Early warning investment became urgent. Floodplain development restrictions tightened. EU Floods Directive implementation accelerated. Climate adaptation funding flowed for wetland restoration and upstream retention basins.

    ### The Human Lesson

    18 people died. 12,000 were evacuated. €4 billion in damage. A motorway collapsed. A nation's primary transport corridor was severed.

    None of this was inevitable. With proper infrastructure for current climate, restored wetlands, coordinated early warning — casualties and damage could have been halved.

    Portugal faces 30–40 more years of intensifying winter storms. The nation can adapt, or suffer repeated 2026-scale catastrophes. This story documents that choice moment.
