# Winter 2025-26 Portuguese Flood Events: A Compound Disaster

How sequential storms, saturated soils, and aging infrastructure created the largest water crisis in a generation.

---

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### The Perfect Storm Sequence

    Between December 31, 2025 and February 8, 2026, Portugal was battered by four major Atlantic windstorms in rapid succession. Each brought rain to already-saturated ground. Each overtopped rivers designed for a climate that no longer exists. When the dust settled, 18 people were dead, 12,000 evacuated, and €4 billion in damage documented. The floods revealed what climate scientists had warned for decades: the old infrastructure assumptions are obsolete.

    This story follows the compound disaster from atmospheric preconditions through peak inundation and into recovery.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 1: The Atlantic Engine Warms

<ScrollytellingBlock>
  <Chapter
    center={[-5, 38]}
    zoom={4}
    datasetId='sst-anomaly'
    layerId='sst-anomaly'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Warm Water Fuels the Storm

    In late January 2026, the Atlantic Ocean off Portugal ran 2-3°C warmer than the 1991-2020 average—a 20-year high. Warm sea surface temperatures (SST) act like an engine, evaporating more moisture into the lower atmosphere and fueling stronger wind systems.

    When Storm Kristin developed in the Atlantic mid-latitudes, it drew energy from this anomalous warmth. The atmosphere held 7% more moisture than it would have in a pre-warming climate. That moisture had to go somewhere.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.5, 39]}
    zoom={7}
    datasetId='soil-moisture-daily'
    layerId='soil-moisture-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-27'
  >
    ### Soil at Maximum Capacity

    Portugal's soils entered January 2026 already saturated. December and early January had delivered 222% of normal rainfall—the 2nd wettest January in the past 26 years. Soil moisture in the upper 7 centimeters (the critical root zone) averaged 0.42 m³/m³, approaching maximum saturation.

    The landscape had no more room for water. Every rain drop from Storm Kristin would flow overland or into rivers. There would be no absorption into groundwater, no delay in runoff. The system was primed for flooding.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-8.5, 39.5]}
    zoom={5}
    datasetId='ivt'
    layerId='ivt'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Atmospheric River Arrives

    Storm Kristin formed part of a larger pattern: an atmospheric river—a corridor of concentrated water vapor in the atmosphere—stretching from the tropics to Portugal. Integrated Water Vapor Transport (IVT) values exceeded 600 kg/m/s in the 2-4 days before landfall, classifying this as an extreme atmospheric river.

    Rivers in the air carry as much water as the Amazon River on the ground. When they make landfall and encounter topography, all that moisture precipitates in a narrow zone. Portugal would experience extreme rainfall rates compressed into 24-48 hour windows.
  </Chapter>
</ScrollytellingBlock>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Compounding Cascades

    SST anomaly + saturated soils + atmospheric river = compound disaster. Each element alone would be notable. Together, they created a system incapable of handling the moisture arrival. Hydrologists call this "compound and cascading flooding"—when multiple hazards interact to amplify impacts beyond simple addition.

    By January 27, 2026, all three preconditions were present. Storm Kristin was 24 hours from landfall.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 2: Storm Kristin — The Record-Breaking Wind

<ScrollytellingBlock>
  <Chapter
    center={[-8.5, 40]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Unprecedented Wind Records

    Storm Kristin made landfall in Leiria district on January 28, 2026, evening. What followed was the most destructive wind event in Portugal since Hurricane Leslie (2018).

    Wind gusts reached:
    - **209.8 km/h** in Soure (Coimbra)—an unprecedented national record
    - **156 km/h** in Leiria (general expectation exceeded)
    - **115 km/h** in Madeira

    Meteorologically, 209 km/h is extreme hurricane-force wind. Structurally designed buildings can survive it, but infrastructure designed for 120 km/h (the pre-climate-change standard) fails catastrophically.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-8.5, 40]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='mslp'
    layerId='mslp'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Pressure Gradient Intensity

    Kristin's central pressure dropped to 953 hPa—a deep low-pressure system. The pressure gradient (difference between low-pressure center and surrounding high-pressure ridge) drove the extreme wind speeds. Meteorologically, this was one of the most intense Atlantic windstorms to reach Portugal in modern records.

    Deep pressure + saturated soils + vegetation-covered terrain = perfect recipe for wind damage. Wet soil cannot anchor tree roots. Trees were uprooted across Coimbra, Leiria, and Guarda districts, creating cascading failures: trees into power lines, trees into buildings, downed trees blocking roads and preventing emergency response.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.2, 38.8]}
    zoom={8}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-30'
  >
    ### Rainfall Follows Wind

    Wind damage was immediate (24-48 hours). Rainfall persisted for 2-3 days, delivering 50-100+ mm across central Portugal. Coimbra recorded significant totals; smaller streams swelled into torrents.

    Because soils were saturated, this rain ran off directly into rivers. The Mondego, Tejo, Vouga, and Sado all responded rapidly to upstream rainfall. River levels began rising on January 28 and climbed through January 30.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.2, 38.8]}
    zoom={8}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-30'
  >
    ### Tejo River Peaks (First Time)

    By January 30, Tejo river discharge reached **3,706 m³/s** at Vila Franca de Xira (the primary gauge). Design capacity for managed flood risk is ~2,000 m³/s. This peak exceeded design by 85%.

    Orange alert levels were activated. Municipalities along the Lezíria do Tejo floodplain (Benavente, Coruche, Cartaxo, Almeirim) prepared evacuation shelters and sandbagging operations. But the crisis was just beginning.
  </Chapter>
</ScrollytellingBlock>

## Chapter 3: Human Cost of Kristin

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Deaths and Destruction

    Storm Kristin killed **14 people** in Portugal (6 direct, 8 indirect):

    **Direct causes:**
    - Falling trees (4 deaths)
    - Woman swept away by floodwater in Algarve (1 death)
    - Structural collapse (1 death)

    **Indirect causes:**
    - Carbon monoxide poisoning from generators used during power outages (2 deaths)
    - Operations and recovery accidents (5 deaths)
    - Mental health crisis (1 suicide)

    ### Power & Infrastructure Impacts

    - **1 million people** lost power (out of Portugal's 10.5M population)
    - **Power outages lasted 5-15 days** in worst-affected regions
    - Hospitals, water treatment plants, and emergency services shifted to backup generators
    - Communication networks degraded (cell towers down, landlines cut)

    ### Economic Damage Assessment

    Kristin alone caused an estimated €2+ billion in damage (infrastructure, housing, forestry). When combined with Leonardo and Marta, the total reached €4+ billion—making this the costliest natural disaster sequence for Portugal in the 21st century.
  </Prose>

## Chapter 4: Storm Leonardo — The Persistent Rainfall

<ScrollytellingBlock>
  <Chapter
    center={[-9.5, 39]}
    zoom={7}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-02-03'
  >
    ### A Different Threat Arrives

    Storm Leonardo formed on February 2, 2026, and made landfall on the Iberian Peninsula on February 3. Unlike Kristin's dramatic wind event, Leonardo brought something more insidious: persistent, heavy rainfall lasting 3-4 days.

    Rainfall rates were moderate (20-40 mm/day), not extreme. But the duration mattered. Three consecutive days of rain on soils already saturated from Kristin meant that every drop flowed into rivers. There was no time for rivers to recede between storms.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.2, 38.8]}
    zoom={8}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-02-07'
  >
    ### Tejo Reaches Maximum

    As Leonardo's rainfall persisted from February 4-6, the Tejo river climbed again. Spanish dam operators (Alcántara and Cedillo dams on the Tagus/Tejo Spanish section) released stored water downstream to prevent dam overtopping, adding to Portuguese inflows.

    On **February 7, 2026, Tejo discharge reached 6,775 m³/s**—the second-highest peak in modern records (exceeded only by some historical events).

    **Design capacity exceeded by 238%.**

    This is a 1-in-100+ year event by traditional hydrology standards. In a warming climate, it will become a 1-in-30 year event by 2050.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-8.9, 38.7]}
    zoom={9}
    datasetId='soil-moisture-daily'
    layerId='soil-moisture-daily'
    datetime='2026-02-08'
  >
    ### The Floodplain Inundates

    By February 8, the Lezíria do Tejo floodplain was underwater across an area of **226,764 hectares**. Salvaterra de Magos alone accounted for 49,164 hectares—an area larger than the city of Lisbon.

    Agricultural fields (rice, vegetables, fruit trees) were submerged. Infrastructure critical for the region's economy (irrigation systems, access roads, bridges) was damaged or destroyed. Thousands of cattle and other livestock perished.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-9.0, 38.5]}
    zoom={7}
    datasetId='sst-anomaly'
    layerId='sst-anomaly'
    showBaseMap={true}
  >
    ### Evacuations Begin

    By February 6, state and local authorities ordered evacuations from low-lying areas. **1,100+ Portuguese residents were evacuated** from municipalities including Alcácer do Sal (Sado basin), Leiria, and surrounding zones.

    These were not brief evacuations. Residents sheltered in schools and emergency centers for 1-2 weeks, unsure when they could return home. Livestock was lost. Agricultural equipment was stranded in fields under water.

    The human cost of evacuations extended beyond immediate danger: psychological trauma, financial loss (many uninsured), disruption of education and work, and social fragmentation of communities.
  </Chapter>
</ScrollytellingBlock>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### The Dike Rupture — Infrastructure Overwhelmed

    On **February 11, 2026**, a critical section of the Mondego River dike near Casais (Soure municipality) ruptured. The rupture was not a surprise to engineers: discharge exceeded 2,100 m³/s, the design capacity set in **1986** for a climate and development baseline that no longer applies.

    The breach inundated agricultural lands immediately downstream and forced evacuation of **3,000 residents** from Coimbra neighborhoods, including Parque Verde.

    This rupture revealed a systemic infrastructure crisis: dikes built 40 years ago cannot handle current climate extremes. Repairs were estimated to take weeks to months. The costs would mount into hundreds of millions of euros.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>

    ### When Infrastructure Fails

    The **A1 motorway collapse on February 12, 2026** symbolized everything wrong with Portugal's climate adaptation response: infrastructure designed for a climate that no longer exists, facing conditions it was never engineered to handle.

    The pillar failure occurred in the Mondego floodplain near Coimbra, just hours after the dike rupture. Floodwaters—carrying debris, scouring foundations, exerting tremendous pressure—undercut the bridge foundation. A single pillar failed. The entire northbound lane collapsed into the floodplain.

    ### The Cascade

    **Impact scope:**
    - **Main transport corridor severed:** Portugal's primary north-south highway (Lisbon ↔ Porto) was closed
    - **Economic disruption:** Businesses depending on transport were crippled
    - **Supply chains:** Food, fuel, medical supplies had to reroute through congested alternatives
    - **Emergency response:** Ambulances and rescue vehicles faced 2-3 hour detours
    - **Recovery cost:** Repairs estimated at €200M+ and several months of closure

    The A1 closure was front-page news internationally. It crystallized the political moment: Portugal's infrastructure, built for a 20th-century climate, could not withstand 21st-century reality.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 6: Storm Marta — The Final Blow

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Sustained Crisis Continues

    Storm Marta arrived on February 6-8, 2026, just as Leonardo was receding. Marta brought additional rainfall (60+ mm in 24 hours in southern mountain areas), strong winds (up to 120 km/h), and rough seas (waves up to 13 meters).

    Most of mainland Portugal (8 of 18 districts) remained on orange alert. The Algarve and Alentejo mountains faced torrential rainfall risk, threatening secondary floods.

    ### The Response Scale

    By February 8, Portugal had deployed **26,500 rescue personnel**—firefighters, military, civil protection officers, volunteers. This was one of the largest emergency mobilizations in recent Portuguese history.

    Rescuers worked 24-hour shifts in dangerous conditions: flooded roads, unstable terrain, hypothermia risk, exhaustion. Two additional deaths occurred during rescue and recovery operations.

    ### Electoral Impact

    The crisis became so severe that **three municipalities postponed Sunday (February 9) presidential elections** because sheltering and emergency response took priority over voting. This had not occurred in Portuguese democracy in decades.
  </Prose>
</Block>

    ### Mapping from Space

    The **Copernicus Emergency Management Service (EMSR864)** activation provided satellite imagery of unprecedented detail. Multiple Sentinel-1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) acquisitions between February 3-16 captured flood extent across 13 Portuguese areas of interest.

    SAR is ideal for flood monitoring because radar penetrates clouds. Unlike optical sensors (Sentinel-2), SAR can "see" through rain and clouds. This enabled frequent, near-daily monitoring during the flooded period.

    EMSR864 delineated **226,764 hectares of inundation** across Portuguese territories. The extent spanned from Aveiro in the north to the Algarve in the south, but concentrated in central basins (Tejo, Mondego, Sado).

    The most extensive inundation occurred in the **Salvaterra de Magos region** (Tejo basin, Santarém district). Peak extent reached **49,164 hectares** on February 8—a single municipality accounting for 21% of total mapped inundation.

    Salvaterra's floodplain is characterized by:
    - **Flat topography:** Little elevation change means water spreads broadly rather than concentrating in channels

    - **Sparse settlement:** Most population on higher ground, but some rural communities in floodplain

## Chapter 8: The Climate Change Fingerprint

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### What Made This Disaster Possible?

    Portugal has experienced floods before. But the 2025-26 winter flooding was distinctly different in scale and character. **Climate change made this disaster more severe and more frequent.**

    ### World Weather Attribution Study

    An international team of climate scientists (World Weather Attribution) analyzed the 2025-26 winter rainfall in the context of climate models. Their findings:

    - **Human-caused climate change increased rainfall intensity by 11%** in northern Portugal
    - **In southern Portugal, the increase was 36%** for extreme one-day rainfall events
    - **Storm Leonardo's one-day rainfall totals are now classified as "once-in-a-century" events** under current climate
    - Without climate change, such totals would be "once-in-200-year" events

    ### The Coimbra Record

    Coimbra city recorded **1,238 mm of rainfall from October 1, 2025 to February 16, 2026**. This is the wettest October-February period in the modern instrumental record, surpassing the previous record set in **1966**.

    The intensity and duration would have been remarkable even without climate change. But climate change made it more likely.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Future Projections for Portugal

    Climate models project for Portugal by 2050:

    - **Annual precipitation will decline 15% (north) to 30% (south)** overall
    - **But extreme precipitation days will intensify**—more intense rain in fewer events
    - **Seasonal shift:** Decreasing rain in spring/summer, but winter rains becoming more extreme
    - **Implication:** More droughts interspersed with catastrophic flooding events

    The 2025-26 winter illustrates this pattern perfectly: months of normal-to-high precipitation (December, January), then sudden extreme rain events (Kristin, Leonardo, Marta) that overwhelm infrastructure.

    ### Policy Relevance

    This climate attribution strengthens the case for:

    1. **PGRI (Flood Risk Management Plans):** Portuguese national flood management framework, now demonstrably inadequate
    2. **EU Floods Directive compliance:** Transboundary coordination with Spain (Tejo/Tagus is shared)
    3. **Infrastructure modernization:** Dikes, drainage systems, motorways need 21st-century design standards
    4. **Ecosystem restoration:** Wetland restoration increases natural flood buffering
    5. **Early warning systems:** Improved precipitation forecasting and automated alerts
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Chapter 10: What cheias.pt Does

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### Monitoring for the Next Flood

    Portugal's hydrological monitoring system (SNIRH) operates 11 primary gauge stations across major basins. Real-time discharge data flows to Proteção Civil (emergency management) and water authorities.

    **cheias.pt** integrates this real-time data with:
    - **Satellite-derived flood extent** (Sentinel-1 SAR, updated 6-12 days)
    - **Rainfall forecasts** (meteorological models)
    - **Soil moisture status** (hydrological models)
    - **Historical context** (previous floods, design thresholds, climate trends)

    The platform enables:

    1. **Early warning:** Weeks in advance when rainfall anomalies develop, days in advance when storms approach
    2. **Situational awareness:** Where is water accumulating? Which basins are at risk? Which populations are in danger?
    3. **Impact estimation:** Based on current conditions + forecast, what is the likely flood extent? What is the expected economic loss?
    4. **Recovery support:** Historical comparisons help communities prepare. Post-flood, satellite maps show extent and guide aid distribution.
    5. **Policy learning:** This story documents what happened. Future storms will be similar (higher intensity, more rapid onset). Infrastructure and policy must adapt.

    ### The Story's Purpose

    By telling this story using satellite data, hydrological monitoring, and climate science, **cheias.pt** transforms raw data into narrative. Data alone is abstract. Story makes it real:

## Epilogue: The New Baseline

<Block>
  <Prose>
    By March 2026, recovery was underway but would take months or years:

    - **A1 motorway:** Repaired by June 2026 (4-month closure)
    - **Dike repairs:** Ongoing through summer and fall 2026
    - **Agricultural recovery:** 2-3 seasons to fully replant and restore productivity
    - **Housing reconstruction:** Many families still sheltered months later
    - **Policy change:** EU and Portuguese climate adaptation funding allocated

    What changed permanently:

    1. **Infrastructure assumptions:** The old design standards (1986 dike capacity, 1970s motorway resilience) are obsolete. New infrastructure must be designed for 2050 climate, not 1990 climate.

    2. **Early warning priority:** Investment in real-time monitoring systems became urgent. cheias.pt and SNIRH funding increased.

    3. **Land use policy:** Floodplain development restrictions were tightened. Building in high-risk zones became more difficult and expensive.

    4. **EU policy:** The 2026 floods accelerated EU Floods Directive implementation in Portugal. Transboundary agreements with Spain were strengthened for coordinated dam management.

    5. **Climate adaptation:** €millions in EU climate adaptation funding flowed to Portugal for wetland restoration, upstream retention basins, and natural flood management.

    ### The Human Lesson

    18 people died. 12,000 were evacuated. €4 billion in damage was documented. A motorway collapsed. A nation's primary transport corridor was severed.

    None of this was inevitable. With proper infrastructure design for current climate, with restored wetlands and upstream water retention, with coordinated early warning and evacuation planning—casualties and damage could have been cut in half or more.

    The 2025-26 floods were a tragedy. They were also a warning that Portugal's (and the world's) climate adaptation work is urgently incomplete. cheias.pt exists to help prevent the next disaster from being equally severe.

    Portugal faces 30-40 more years of similar winter storms as climate change intensifies. The nation can adapt, or it can suffer repeated 2026-scale catastrophes. This story documents that choice moment.
