<Block>
  <Prose>
  Between December 31, 2025 and February 8, 2026, four Atlantic windstorms struck Portugal in rapid succession. Each brought rain to already-saturated ground. Each overtopped rivers designed for a climate that no longer exists. Eighteen people died, 12,000 were evacuated, and €4 billion in damage was documented — the costliest natural disaster sequence in Portugal's 21st-century history.

  This story follows the compound disaster from atmospheric preconditions through peak inundation and into recovery.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## The Atmospheric Setup

<ScrollytellingBlock>
  <Chapter
    center={[-20, 38]}
    zoom={4}
    datasetId='sst-anomaly'
    layerId='sst-anomaly'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### The Atlantic Engine Warms

    In late January 2026, the Atlantic off Portugal ran 2–3°C above the 1991–2020 average — a 20-year high. Warm SSTs evaporate more moisture into the lower atmosphere and fuel stronger cyclones. When Storm Kristin developed in the mid-latitudes, it drew energy from this anomalous warmth. The atmosphere held 7% more moisture than it would have without warming.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-12, 39]}
    zoom={5}
    datasetId='ivt'
    layerId='ivt'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Atmospheric River Makes Landfall

    An atmospheric river — a corridor of concentrated water vapor — stretched from the tropics to Iberia. Integrated Water Vapor Transport exceeded 600 kg/m/s, classifying this as an extreme event. Atmospheric rivers carry as much water as the Amazon. When they hit topography, all that moisture precipitates in a narrow zone over 24–48 hours.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-11, 39.5]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='precipitation-daily'
    layerId='precipitation-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-28'
  >
    ### Kristin Strikes

    Storm Kristin made landfall in Leiria district on January 28, delivering the most destructive wind event since Hurricane Leslie (2018). Gusts reached 209.8 km/h in Soure — an unprecedented national record. Wind-driven rain of 50–100+ mm across central Portugal ran directly off saturated soils into rivers. The Tejo, Mondego, Vouga, and Sado all responded within hours.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-11, 40.5]}
    zoom={6}
    datasetId='soil-moisture-daily'
    layerId='soil-moisture-daily'
    datetime='2026-01-27'
  >
    ### No Room for Water

    Portugal's soils entered January already saturated. December and early January had delivered 222% of normal rainfall. Soil moisture in the upper 7 cm averaged 0.42 m³/m³ — approaching maximum capacity. Every drop from Kristin would flow overland. No absorption, no delay. The system was primed.
  </Chapter>

  <Chapter
    center={[-11, 40.5]}
    zoom={7}
    datasetId='precondition'
    layerId='precondition'
    datetime='2026-01-27'
  >
    ### Compound Risk: Maximum

    Warm SSTs + saturated soils + atmospheric river = compound disaster. The precondition index captures this convergence — all three hazard drivers at simultaneous extremes. By January 27, every component was maxed out. Storm Kristin was 24 hours from landfall. Portugal's flood infrastructure was about to face conditions it was never designed to handle.
  </Chapter>
</ScrollytellingBlock>

## The Storm Sequence

<Block>
  <Prose>
  Seven named storms struck Portugal between December 31 and February 8. Three caused catastrophic damage:

  | Storm | Dates | Max Wind | Deaths | Evacuations | Key Impact |
  |-------|-------|----------|--------|-------------|------------|
  | Kristin | Jan 28–30 | 209 km/h | 14 | — | Worst wind since Leslie 2018; 1M without power |
  | Leonardo | Feb 3–6 | — | 2 | 1,100 | Persistent rainfall; Sado/Lis overflow |
  | Marta | Feb 6–8 | 120 km/h | 2 | 11,000 | Agricultural devastation; 26,500 rescuers deployed |

  Kristin broke wind records. Leonardo broke rivers. Marta broke communities. The compound effect — storms arriving before the landscape could recover — made this sequence historically unprecedented.
  </Prose>
</Block>

  <Prose>
    ### When Infrastructure Fails

    On February 11, the Mondego dike at Casais ruptured — discharge exceeded its 1986 design capacity. The next day, floodwaters undercut the A1 motorway bridge foundation. One pillar failed. The entire northbound lane collapsed into the floodplain.

    Portugal's main highway (Lisbon ↔ Porto) was severed. Supply chains rerouted. Emergency vehicles faced 2–3 hour detours. Repairs cost €200M+ and took four months. The collapse crystallized the political reality: infrastructure built for a 20th-century climate cannot withstand 21st-century storms.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
    ### The Scale of Response

    The compound disaster triggered Portugal's largest emergency mobilization in decades. Storm Kristin alone left one million people without power — some for up to 15 days. As Leonardo and Marta followed, the response escalated: 1,100 evacuated during Leonardo (Alcácer do Sal, Leiria), then 11,000+ during Marta as rivers stayed above flood stage. When the Mondego dike ruptured on February 11, 3,000 more were evacuated from the Coimbra area.

    At peak deployment, 26,500 rescuers were in the field — Proteção Civil, military, and volunteer firefighters working across 69 municipalities under a state of calamity. Three municipalities postponed Sunday elections because shelters had taken priority over polling stations.

    The government announced a €2.5 billion emergency package: housing reconstruction, €750 million for agricultural losses across the Lezíria do Tejo and Baixo Mondego, and A1 motorway repair. Against €4 billion in total documented damage, the gap remained significant — uninsured losses, displaced agricultural workers, and communities waiting months for permanent housing.
  </Prose>
</Block>

<Block>
  <Prose>
  ### What Climate Science Says

  The World Weather Attribution study found human-caused climate change increased rainfall intensity by 11% in northern Portugal and 36% in the south. Leonardo's one-day totals are now "once-in-a-century" events; without warming, they would be "once-in-200-year."

  By 2050, annual precipitation will decline 15–30% overall — but extreme rainfall days will intensify. The pattern: more droughts punctuated by catastrophic floods. The 2025–26 winter is not an anomaly. It is the new baseline.
  </Prose>
</Block>

## Recovery and the Road Ahead

<Block>
  <Prose>
  By mid-February, river levels dropped. The acute crisis ended — but recovery will take years.

  The government announced a €2.5 billion emergency package: housing reconstruction grants, €750M for agricultural losses, A1 motorway repair. But against €4 billion in total damage, significant gaps remain — uninsured losses, psychological support, livelihood transition for displaced communities.

  What changed permanently: infrastructure assumptions from 1986 are obsolete. Dikes, drainage systems, and motorways must be redesigned for 2050 climate. The EU Floods Directive gained new urgency. Transboundary agreements with Spain on Tejo/Tagus dam management were strengthened. And investment in real-time monitoring — the kind of system cheias.pt provides — became an acknowledged national priority.

  Eighteen people died. None of it was inevitable. With infrastructure designed for current climate, restored wetlands for upstream retention, and coordinated early warning, casualties and damage could have been halved. Portugal faces 30–40 more years of similar winter storms. This story documents the choice moment.
  </Prose>
</Block>
